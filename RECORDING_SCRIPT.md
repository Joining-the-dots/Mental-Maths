# Learning Lab — voice recording script

The game plays YOUR voice wherever it finds a recording, and only falls back
to the robot voice when a file is missing — so you can record in any order,
a few at a time, and the game upgrades as you go.

## How to record

1. Quiet room, phone Voice Memos (or any recorder) is fine.
2. **One file per item.** Read the whole passage naturally — like you are
   explaining it at the kitchen table, not reading the news. A bit of energy
   (tecmath-style enthusiasm) makes a huge difference.
3. Save/export each file with **exactly** the filename shown (lowercase),
   as `.mp3` or `.m4a` (both work — Voice Memos exports .m4a).
4. Drop the files into the game folder: lesson files in `audio/tricks/`,
   spelling files in `audio/words/`. Push to GitHub like any other file.
5. The whiteboard paces its writing to your recording, so natural short
   pauses between sentences work beautifully.

# Part 1 — Maths trick lessons (59 recordings, ~30-60 seconds each)

## Addition

### 🎙️ `audio/tricks/add-leftright.mp3` — Left to Right

> Left to Right means you add the BIGGEST parts first.
> Take 34 + 52. Split each number into tens and ones.
> Add the tens first: 30 + 50 = 80.
> Then add the ones: 4 + 2 = 6.
> Put them together: 80 + 6 = 86. Always start with the tens!

### 🎙️ `audio/tricks/add-bonds.mp3` — Number Bonds

> Number bonds are pairs of numbers that make 10.
> 7 + 3 = 10. 6 + 4 = 10. 8 + 2 = 10. 5 + 5 = 10.
> Learn them so you never have to count them out.
> They work for 100 too: 70 + 30, 60 + 40, 80 + 20.
> Knowing your bonds makes every other trick faster.

### 🎙️ `audio/tricks/add-bigger.mp3` — Bigger Number First

> Adding is easier if you start with the BIGGER number.
> For 3 + 28, do not start at 3.
> Flip it round: 28 + 3. The answer is exactly the same.
> Now just count on from 28: 29, 30, 31.
> So 3 + 28 = 31.

### 🎙️ `audio/tricks/add-counton.mp3` — Count On

> When you add 1, 2 or 3, do not work it out — just count up.
> 46 + 3: start at 46… 47, 48, 49.
> Three little hops. 46 + 3 = 49.
> It works counting on from any number.

### 🎙️ `audio/tricks/add-doubles.mp3` — Doubles

> A double is a number added to itself.
> 6 + 6 = 12. 7 + 7 = 14. 8 + 8 = 16. 9 + 9 = 18.
> Learn them off by heart — then they are instant.
> Doubles are the anchors that lots of other sums lean on.

### 🎙️ `audio/tricks/add-neardoubles.mp3` — Near Doubles

> A near double is ALMOST a double.
> 6 + 7 is nearly 6 + 6.
> Double the 6 gives 12, then add the extra 1 gives 13.
> So 6 + 7 = 13.
> Tip: for numbers 2 apart (like 7 + 9), double the MIDDLE one — double 8 = 16.

### 🎙️ `audio/tricks/add-maketen.mp3` — Make Ten

> Make Ten means jumping to the next ten first.
> 8 + 5: how many gets 8 up to 10? That is 2.
> Split the 5 into 2 + 3.
> 8 + 2 = 10, then 10 + 3 = 13.
> So 8 + 5 = 13.

### 🎙️ `audio/tricks/add-spottens.mp3` — Spot the Tens

> When you add lots of numbers, look for pairs that make 10.
> Take 6 + 3 + 4 + 7.
> Spot them: 6 + 4 = 10, and 3 + 7 = 10.
> Add the tens: 10 + 10 = 20.
> So 6 + 3 + 4 + 7 = 20.

### 🎙️ `audio/tricks/add-roundadjust.mp3` — Round and Adjust

> Round one number UP to something tidy, then fix it afterwards.
> 38 + 27: round 38 up to 40 — that is 2 extra.
> Now the easy sum: 40 + 27 = 67.
> Take back the 2 you added: 67 minus 2 = 65.
> So 38 + 27 = 65.

### 🎙️ `audio/tricks/add-givetake.mp3` — Give and Take

> Give and Take keeps the total exactly the same.
> 47 + 26: give 3 from the 26 across to the 47.
> The 47 becomes 50. The 26 becomes 23.
> Now it is easy: 50 + 23 = 73.
> So 47 + 26 = 73.

### 🎙️ `audio/tricks/add-add9.mp3` — Add 9 and 99

> Adding 9 is nearly the same as adding 10.
> 56 + 9: add 10 gives 66. Then take 1 back gives 65.
> So 56 + 9 = 65.
> Adding 99? Add 100, then take 1 back.
> 56 + 99: 56 + 100 = 156, then minus 1 = 155.

## Subtraction

### 🎙️ `audio/tricks/sub-takeaway.mp3` — Take Away in Parts

> Take away the big parts first, then the small parts.
> 56 minus 23: first take away the 20.
> 56 minus 20 = 36.
> Now take away the 3: 36 minus 3 = 33.
> So 56 minus 23 = 33.

### 🎙️ `audio/tricks/sub-countback.mp3` — Count Back

> When you take away 1, 2 or 3, do not work it out — count down.
> 49 minus 3: 48, 47, 46.
> Three little steps back. 49 minus 3 = 46.

### 🎙️ `audio/tricks/sub-countup.mp3` — Count Up

> If two numbers are close, count UP from the smaller one.
> 63 minus 58: start at 58… 59, 60, 61, 62, 63.
> That is 5 steps, so 63 minus 58 = 5.
> Counting up is far easier than counting back here.

### 🎙️ `audio/tricks/sub-bridgedown.mp3` — Bridge Down

> Bridge Down means stepping onto the ten first.
> 52 minus 7: how many gets you down to 50? That is 2.
> Split the 7 into 2 + 5.
> 52 minus 2 = 50, then 50 minus 5 = 45.
> So 52 minus 7 = 45.

### 🎙️ `audio/tricks/sub-thinkadd.mp3` — Think Addition

> Every subtraction is an addition in disguise.
> For 15 minus 8, ask: 8 + ? = 15.
> 8 + 7 = 15, so 15 minus 8 = 7.
> Use the addition facts you already know.

### 🎙️ `audio/tricks/sub-roundadjust.mp3` — Round and Adjust

> Round the number you are taking away to something tidy.
> 73 minus 38: round 38 up to 40.
> 73 minus 40 = 33.
> You took away 2 too many, so add 2 back: 33 + 2 = 35.
> So 73 minus 38 = 35.

### 🎙️ `audio/tricks/sub-samediff.mp3` — Same Difference

> The difference does not change if you shift BOTH numbers the same way.
> 63 minus 28: add 2 to each gives 65 minus 30.
> 65 minus 30 = 35.
> So 63 minus 28 = 35. Much tidier!

### 🎙️ `audio/tricks/sub-subfrom100.mp3` — Subtract from 100

> To subtract from 100, find the pair that makes 100.
> 100 minus 63: 63 + 37 = 100.
> So 100 minus 63 = 37.
> Tip: if the number does NOT end in 0, the ones digits add to 10 and the tens digits add to 9. Ends in 0 (like 100 minus 40)? Just use tens bonds: 4 + 6 = 10, so the answer is 60.

### 🎙️ `audio/tricks/sub-sub9.mp3` — Subtract 9 and 99

> Taking away 9 is nearly taking away 10.
> 64 minus 9: take away 10 gives 54. Give 1 back gives 55.
> So 64 minus 9 = 55.
> Taking away 99? Take away 100, then give 1 back.

## Division

### 🎙️ `audio/tricks/div-inverse.mp3` — Times Tables Backwards

> Division is the OPPOSITE of multiplication.
> 56 divided by 7 is just asking: "7 times what makes 56?"
> 7 times 8 = 56, so 56 divided by 7 = 8.
> If your times tables are sharp, division is instant — every division fact is just a times-table fact flipped.

### 🎙️ `audio/tricks/div-div10.mp3` — ÷ 10, 100, 1000

> To divide by 10, take ONE zero off the end: 470 divided by 10 = 47.
> To divide by 100, take TWO zeros off: 4700 divided by 100 = 47.
> To divide by 1000, take THREE zeros off: 47000 divided by 1000 = 47.
> Tip: it is really the decimal point moving LEFT — one place for each zero in the divisor.

### 🎙️ `audio/tricks/div-rule2510.mp3` — The 2, 5, 10 Test

> You can tell at a glance if a number divides by 2, 5 or 10:
> , divided by 2: last digit is 0, 2, 4, 6 or 8 (even).
> , divided by 5: last digit is 0 or 5.
> , divided by 10: last digit is 0.
> So 346 divides by 2 (ends in 6). 345 divides by 5 (ends in 5). 340 divides by all three.
> No actual division needed — just peek at the last digit.

### 🎙️ `audio/tricks/div-rule39.mp3` — Digit Sum for 3 and 9

> For 3: if the digits add to a multiple of 3, the number divides by 3.
> 4-7-1: 4 + 7 + 1 = 12. 12 divided by 3 = 4, so YES — 471 divides by 3.
> For 9: if the digits add to a multiple of 9, the number divides by 9.
> 5-4-9: 5 + 4 + 9 = 18. 18 divided by 9 = 2, so YES — 549 divides by 9.
> Trick within a trick: keep adding the digits of the digit-sum until you have one digit. If it is 9, the number divides by 9.

### 🎙️ `audio/tricks/div-div4.mp3` — ÷ 4 = Halve Twice

> Dividing by 4 is the same as halving TWICE.
> 96 divided by 4: half of 96 is 48. Half of 48 is 24.
> So 96 divided by 4 = 24.
> Halving is usually easier in your head than divided by 4 in one go.
> Works because 4 = 2 times 2.

### 🎙️ `audio/tricks/div-div8.mp3` — ÷ 8 = Halve Three Times

> Dividing by 8 is halving THREE times in a row.
> 120 divided by 8: half is 60. Half of 60 is 30. Half of 30 is 15.
> So 120 divided by 8 = 15.
> Three quick halves and you are done.
> Works because 8 = 2 times 2 times 2.

### 🎙️ `audio/tricks/div-div5.mp3` — ÷ 5 = Double then ÷ 10

> Dividing by 5 is the same as doubling, then dividing by 10.
> 630 divided by 5: double 630 gives 1260. divided by 10 gives 126.
> So 630 divided by 5 = 126.
> Why? Because 1 over 5 = 2 over 10.
> You can do it in either order — double first or divided by 10 first — pick whichever looks easier.

### 🎙️ `audio/tricks/div-rule48.mp3` — Last Two for 4, Last Three for 8

> For divided by 4: look ONLY at the last two digits. If THEY divide by 4, the whole number does too.
> 7,316: last two are 16. 16 divided by 4 = 4, so 7,316 divides by 4.
> For divided by 8: look ONLY at the last three digits. If they divide by 8, the whole number does.
> 5,128: last three are 128. 128 divided by 8 = 16, so 5,128 divides by 8.
> Saves you from dividing the whole big number.

### 🎙️ `audio/tricks/div-simplify.mp3` — Halve Both First

> If the dividend AND divisor are both even, halve them both first.
> 164 divided by 26: both even. Halve both gives 82 divided by 13.
> 82 divided by 13 = 6 remainder 4. The 6 — the whole-number answer — is the same. But careful: any REMAINDER got halved with everything else, so double it back: 164 divided by 26 = 6 remainder 8.
> You can keep halving as long as both are even.
> 320 divided by 16 gives 160 divided by 8 gives 80 divided by 4 gives 40 divided by 2 gives 20. All the same answer: 20.

### 🎙️ `audio/tricks/div-chunk.mp3` — Split the Dividend

> Split the BIG number (the dividend) into chunks that each divide cleanly.
> 864 divided by 6: split 864 into 600 + 264.
> 600 divided by 6 = 100. 264 divided by 6 = 44.
> Add the answers: 100 + 44 = 144.
> So 864 divided by 6 = 144. Two easy divisions instead of one hard one.

### 🎙️ `audio/tricks/div-leftright.mp3` — Front-End First

> Mental division is easier from LEFT to right.
> 256 divided by 8: ask first — about how many tens? 8 times 30 = 240, that fits.
> So the answer starts with 30-something.
> Now what is left: 256 minus 240 = 16. 16 divided by 8 = 2.
> Put them together: 30 + 2 = 32. So 256 divided by 8 = 32.

### 🎙️ `audio/tricks/div-partial.mp3` — Subtract Friendly Chunks

> Build the answer up by SUBTRACTING easy multiples of the divisor.
> 432 divided by 6: start big. 6 times 50 = 300. Take it off: 432 minus 300 = 132.
> 6 times 20 = 120. Take it off: 132 minus 120 = 12.
> 6 times 2 = 12. Take it off: 12 minus 12 = 0.
> Add the friendly multipliers: 50 + 20 + 2 = 72. So 432 divided by 6 = 72.

### 🎙️ `audio/tricks/div-estimate.mp3` — Round to Estimate

> When the numbers are awkward, ROUND them to something tidier.
> 347 divided by 6: round 347 to 360 (a multiple of 6).
> 360 divided by 6 = 60. So the answer is about 60.
> That tells you the size of the answer — and stops silly mistakes.
> Always estimate before you start a big division — you will spot a wrong answer straight away.

### 🎙️ `audio/tricks/div-rule11.mp3` — The 11 Test

> For divisibility by 11, ADD and SUBTRACT the digits alternately.
> 2-9-1-5: 2 minus 9 + 1 minus 5 = minus 11. minus 11 divided by 11 = minus 1, so YES — 2915 divided by 11 = 265.
> If the alternating sum is 0 or any multiple of 11, the number divides by 11.
> Try 1-3-2: 1 minus 3 + 2 = 0 gives YES, 132 divided by 11 = 12.
> Otherwise: remember 11s as 11 times 12 = 132, 11 times 13 = 143, 11 times 14 = 154…

### 🎙️ `audio/tricks/div-rule7.mp3` — The 7 Trick

> For divisibility by 7: DOUBLE the last digit and SUBTRACT it from the rest.
> 364: last digit 4, double = 8. Rest = 36. 36 minus 8 = 28. 28 divided by 7 = 4, so YES.
> Then 364 divided by 7 = 52.
> If the test number is small enough, you can see straight away whether it divides by 7.
> For most everyday sevens, just lean on your 7 times table — it is the most-tested fact in this whole topic.

## Fractions

### 🎙️ `audio/tricks/frac-samebottom.mp3` — Same Bottom → Add the Tops

> If two fractions have the SAME bottom number, leave the bottom alone and just add (or subtract) the tops.
> 2 over 7 + 3 over 7: bottoms match. Add the tops: 2 + 3 = 5. Answer: 5 over 7.
> 5 over 9 minus 1 over 9: bottoms match. Subtract: 5 minus 1 = 4. Answer: 4 over 9.
> Everything else with fractions builds on this single rule.

### 🎙️ `audio/tricks/frac-memorise.mp3` — Magic Fraction Facts

> A handful of fraction facts make EVERYTHING faster. Memorise these:
> 1 over 2 = 0.5 = 50 per cent. 1 over 4 = 0.25 = 25 per cent. 3 over 4 = 0.75 = 75 per cent.
> 1 over 5 = 0.2 = 20 per cent. 2 over 5 = 0.4 = 40 per cent. 1 over 10 = 0.1 = 10 per cent.
> 1 over 3 is about 0.33 is about 33 per cent. 2 over 3 is about 0.67 is about 67 per cent.
> 1 over 8 = 0.125 = 12.5 per cent.
> Once you know these, you barely have to calculate.

### 🎙️ `audio/tricks/frac-fracof.mp3` — Fraction of a Number

> To find a fraction OF a number: DIVIDE by the bottom, then MULTIPLY by the top.
> 3 over 4 of 20: divide by 4 gives 5. Multiply by 3 gives 15. So 3 over 4 of 20 = 15.
> 2 over 3 of 18: divided by 3 = 6. times 2 = 12. So 2 over 3 of 18 = 12.
> Always do the divided by first — it keeps the numbers small.

### 🎙️ `audio/tricks/frac-simplifyfrac.mp3` — Simplify by Halving

> If the top AND bottom are both even, halve them both. Keep going until you cannot any more.
> 12 over 16: both even. Halve gives 6 over 8. Halve again gives 3 over 4. That is fully simplified.
> If both end in 0, you can save a step by slashing the zeros (see "Slash the Zeros").
> Halving is the easiest place to start — try it before any other simplifying.

### 🎙️ `audio/tricks/frac-slashzeros.mp3` — Slash the Zeros

> If the top AND bottom both end in 0, you can SLASH a zero off each.
> 30 over 50: cross out the zeros gives 3 over 5. Done.
> 120 over 200: slash one zero from each gives 12 over 20. Slash again gives no, only the 20 still ends in a zero. Use halving instead: 12 over 20 gives 6 over 10 gives 3 over 5.
> Slashing zeros is just dividing top and bottom by 10. Same idea, much faster to write.

### 🎙️ `audio/tricks/frac-benchmark.mp3` — Compare to One Half

> Halfway is when the TOP is exactly half the BOTTOM.
> 4 over 8 is exactly a half (because 4 is half of 8).
> 3 over 8 is LESS than a half (3 is less than half of 8).
> 5 over 8 is MORE than a half (5 is more than half of 8).
> This is the quickest way to compare a fraction to 1 over 2 — no maths needed, just a quick look.

### 🎙️ `audio/tricks/frac-crossmult.mp3` — Cross-Multiply to Compare

> To compare two fractions, cross-multiply: top times OTHER bottom.
> 2 over 3 vs 5 over 8: 2 times 8 = 16. 5 times 3 = 15.
> 16 is bigger, and 16 came from 2 over 3 — so 2 over 3 is the bigger fraction.
> It works because you are really making a common denominator without writing it down.
> Whichever PRODUCT is biggest, that side wins.

### 🎙️ `audio/tricks/frac-cancelmul.mp3` — Cancel Before Multiplying

> When you multiply fractions, look for shared factors and SLASH them out FIRST.
> 3 over 4 times 8 over 9: the 3 and 9 share a 3 gives cancel to 1 and 3. The 4 and 8 share a 4 gives cancel to 1 and 2.
> You are left with 1 over 1 times 2 over 3 = 2 over 3.
> Cancelling FIRST keeps the numbers small. Way easier than multiplying first and simplifying after.
> Tip: cancel any TOP with any BOTTOM — diagonally, or within the same fraction. Never top-with-top or bottom-with-bottom.

### 🎙️ `audio/tricks/frac-kcf.mp3` — Keep, Change, Flip

> To divide by a fraction: KEEP the first, CHANGE divided by to times , FLIP the second.
> 1 over 2 divided by 1 over 4: keep 1 over 2. Change divided by to times . Flip 1 over 4 gives 4 over 1.
> Now multiply: 1 over 2 times 4 over 1 = 4 over 2 = 2. So 1 over 2 divided by 1 over 4 = 2.
> Why? Dividing by 1 over 4 is the same as asking "how many quarters fit?" — and multiplying by 4 gives the same answer.
> It always works: KEEP — CHANGE — FLIP.

### 🎙️ `audio/tricks/frac-butterfly.mp3` — Butterfly Method

> For adding fractions with DIFFERENT bottoms, draw a butterfly:
> 2 over 3 + 1 over 4: multiply the wings — 2 times 4 = 8, and 1 times 3 = 3. Top of answer = 8 + 3 = 11.
> Multiply the bottoms for the new bottom: 3 times 4 = 12.
> So 2 over 3 + 1 over 4 = 11 over 12.
> No need to find a common denominator first — the butterfly does it for you.

### 🎙️ `audio/tricks/frac-commondenom.mp3` — Spot the Easy Common Bottom

> Before reaching for the butterfly, check: is one bottom a MULTIPLE of the other?
> 1 over 4 + 3 over 8: 8 is a multiple of 4. So just change 1 over 4 to 2 over 8.
> Now both bottoms match: 2 over 8 + 3 over 8 = 5 over 8. Done.
> Always look for the easy common bottom first. Butterfly is the back-up plan.

### 🎙️ `audio/tricks/frac-fractopct.mp3` — Fraction → Percent

> Per cent means "out of 100". So to turn a fraction into a percentage, make the bottom 100.
> 3 over 5: multiply top and bottom by 20 gives 60 over 100. That is 60 per cent.
> 7 over 20: multiply both by 5 gives 35 over 100. That is 35 per cent.
> If the bottom is not a factor of 100, divide top by bottom on paper to get the decimal, then times 100.

## Percentages

### 🎙️ `audio/tricks/pct-find10.mp3` — 10% — Shift the Decimal

> 10 per cent means "1 out of every 10" — divide by 10.
> Quickest way: move the decimal point ONE place to the LEFT.
> 10 per cent of 47 gives 4.7. 10 per cent of 360 gives 36. 10 per cent of 8 gives 0.8.
> This is the single most important percentage trick. Almost every other percentage builds from here.

### 🎙️ `audio/tricks/pct-find1.mp3` — 1% — Shift TWICE

> 1 per cent means "1 out of every 100" — divide by 100.
> Move the decimal point TWO places to the LEFT.
> 1 per cent of 300 gives 3. 1 per cent of 47 gives 0.47. 1 per cent of 1200 gives 12.
> Combine with 10 per cent and you can build any percentage at all.

### 🎙️ `audio/tricks/pct-pctfrac.mp3` — % ↔ Fraction Magic

> These are worth knowing by heart — they make per cent problems instant:
> 50 per cent = 1 over 2. 25 per cent = 1 over 4. 75 per cent = 3 over 4.
> 20 per cent = 1 over 5. 10 per cent = 1 over 10. 1 per cent = 1 over 100.
> 12.5 per cent = 1 over 8. 33 per cent is about 1 over 3. 66 per cent is about 2 over 3.
> "25 per cent off pounds 80" is the same as " divided by 4" gives pounds 20 off. Much faster than calculating.

### 🎙️ `audio/tricks/pct-build.mp3` — Build from 10, 5 and 1

> You can build ANY percentage from a few 10 per cents, 5 per cents and 1 per cents.
> 15 per cent of 60: 10 per cent is 6. 5 per cent is half of that gives 3. Add gives 6 + 3 = 9.
> 23 per cent of 200: 10 per cent is 20. 20 per cent is 40. 1 per cent is 2. 3 per cent is 6. Add gives 40 + 6 = 46.
> Always start with 10 per cent — then halve, double, or add 1 per cents to land on your number.

### 🎙️ `audio/tricks/pct-halfdouble.mp3` — Halve and Double

> Once you have 10 per cent, two quick moves give you a lot more:
> 5 per cent = HALVE the 10 per cent.
> 20 per cent = DOUBLE the 10 per cent.
> 2.5 per cent = HALVE the 5 per cent.
> 5 per cent of 80: 10 per cent is 8. Halve gives 4. 20 per cent of 80: 10 per cent is 8. Double gives 16.
> Most everyday percentages are reached in 1 or 2 halvings or doublings.

### 🎙️ `audio/tricks/pct-swap.mp3` — Swap the Numbers

> A magical fact: x per cent of y is ALWAYS the same as y per cent of x.
> So 4 per cent of 50 = 50 per cent of 4 = 2. Way easier the second way.
> 12 per cent of 25 = 25 per cent of 12 = 3 (a quarter of 12).
> Whenever the percentage looks awkward, try SWAPPING — sometimes a horrible question becomes an easy one.

### 🎙️ `audio/tricks/pct-isofov.mp3` — "Is over Of"

> To work out what PERCENT one number is of another:
> (part divided by whole) times 100.
> 30 out of 40 questions right? 30 divided by 40 = 0.75. times 100 = 75 per cent.
> 12 out of 50? 12 divided by 50 = 0.24. times 100 = 24 per cent.
> Useful tip: think "IS over OF". "What percent IS 30 OF 40?" gives 30 over 40.

### 🎙️ `audio/tricks/pct-multiplier.mp3` — Multiplier Method (+% or −%)

> To ADD a percentage, multiply by 1 + ( per cent as a decimal).
> +25 per cent gives times 1.25. +10 per cent gives times 1.1. +50 per cent gives times 1.5.
> To TAKE OFF a percentage, multiply by 1 minus ( per cent as a decimal).
> minus 25 per cent gives times 0.75. minus 10 per cent gives times 0.9. minus 50 per cent gives times 0.5.
> Example: pounds 80 + 25 per cent gives 80 times 1.25 = pounds 100. pounds 80 minus 25 per cent gives 80 times 0.75 = pounds 60.
> One multiply, no add-on step. Fast.

### 🎙️ `audio/tricks/pct-chain.mp3` — Chain Two %s with Multipliers

> For TWO percentage changes, multiply the two multipliers.
> +10 per cent then minus 15 per cent: times 1.10 times 0.85 = times 0.935.
> So the total change is a 6.5 per cent DROP — not 5 per cent as you might guess.
> A 20 per cent rise followed by a 20 per cent fall is times 1.2 times 0.8 = times 0.96 — a 4 per cent loss overall.
> Useful for sales, taxes, interest, anything that stacks.

### 🎙️ `audio/tricks/pct-reverse.mp3` — Reverse Percentages

> If you only know the answer AFTER a percentage change, you can work back.
> After 50 per cent off, the sale price is pounds 45. What was the original?
> pounds 45 is 50 per cent of the original, so original = pounds 45 divided by 50 times 100 = pounds 90.
> Or just multiply by 2 (because 50 per cent = half).
> Rule: divide the new amount by the per cent LEFT (as a decimal). pounds 60 = 75 per cent left gives 60 divided by 0.75 = pounds 80 original.

### 🎙️ `audio/tricks/pct-tips.mp3` — Tip Combos

> Restaurant tip maths is just three combinations:
> 15 per cent = 10 per cent + 5 per cent (half of the 10 per cent added on).
> 20 per cent = 10 per cent times 2.
> 18 per cent = 20 per cent minus 2 per cent (or just round up from 15 per cent).
> A pounds 45 bill: 10 per cent is pounds 4.50. 20 per cent is pounds 9. 15 per cent is pounds 4.50 + pounds 2.25 = pounds 6.75.
> Once you know 10 per cent, every tip is a few seconds of mental maths.

### 🎙️ `audio/tricks/pct-fracpct.mp3` — Use a Fraction for Tidy %s

> Some percentages are MUCH faster as fractions.
> 60 per cent of 35: 60 per cent = 3 over 5. 3 over 5 of 35 = 21.
> 25 per cent of 48: 25 per cent = 1 over 4. 48 divided by 4 = 12.
> 75 per cent of 80: 75 per cent = 3 over 4. 80 divided by 4 times 3 = 60.
> Whenever the percentage is 10, 20, 25, 50, 75 (and the number divides nicely), the fraction route is fastest.
